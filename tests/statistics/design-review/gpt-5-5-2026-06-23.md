# 3. Potential confounds

## A. Cage effects are a major concern

You list cage effects and plan 2 cages per group. This is probably the biggest design problem.

Microbiome studies in mice are heavily affected by cage because cage-mates share microbes through coprophagy, grooming, bedding, and environmental exposure. Individual mice within a cage are not fully independent for microbiome outcomes.

With only 2 cages per group, your effective sample size for microbiome composition may be closer to 2 independent units per group, not 12 mice per EAE group.

This creates a risk that a “diet effect” is actually a cage effect.

For example:

- One high-fiber cage randomly starts with more Bacteroidetes.
- One standard-diet cage has a bloom of pathobionts.
- PERMANOVA detects a “diet effect,” but it is really cage clustering.

### Recommendation

Increase number of cages per group and reduce mice per cage if necessary.

For microbiome work, 4–6 cages per group is generally much safer than 2 cages per group.

For example, instead of:

- 12 mice in 2 cages = 6 mice/cage

Prefer:

- 12 mice in 4 cages = 3 mice/cage  
or
- 15 mice in 5 cages = 3 mice/cage

Then include cage as a random effect or constrain permutation by cage where appropriate.

**Essential fix.**

You mention litter effects but do not describe how you will handle them.

Mice from the same litter share early-life microbiota and maternal effects. If all pups from one litter are assigned disproportionately to one diet, litter becomes confounded with treatment.

### Recommendation

Distribute littermates across groups where feasible.

For example:

- From each litter, assign some mice to standard diet and some to high-fiber diet.
- Balance EAE and no-EAE groups across litters if possible.
- Include litter as a random effect if litter information is available.

However, assigning littermates to different diets means they must be separated into different cages after weaning, which introduces cage effects. That is manageable, but must be planned carefully.

**Essential to address in randomization.**

#### 3. Food intake/body weight differences

High-fiber diet could reduce disease through caloric restriction or altered body condition rather than microbiome-mediated immune regulation.

Microbiome differences after disease onset may reflect disease severity rather than cause it.

If high-fiber and standard-diet mice are immunized at different times, by different handlers, or with different emulsion batches, this could confound results.

- Randomize immunization order across groups.
- Use the same MOG/CFA emulsion batch where possible.
- Balance mice from all groups across induction sessions.

- Track body weight and food intake.
- Pre-specify primary endpoint.

# 5. Statistical concerns

## A. Sample size justification is too vague

You say n=10 has been adequate in previous EAE studies. That may be true for clinical EAE scores, but it may not be adequate for microbiome endpoints or immune phenotyping.

Also, your groups are unequal:

- EAE groups: n=12
- No-EAE groups: n=6

That is acceptable if the main clinical comparison is between EAE groups, but it limits diet comparisons in healthy controls.

### Concerns

- EAE variability can be high.
- Some mice may fail to develop disease.
- Some may reach humane endpoint before day 21.
- Microbiome analyses are often underpowered with low cage replication.
- Flow cytometry may have tissue-processing failures.

### Recommendation

Perform a power calculation based on a specific primary endpoint, such as:

- Mean peak EAE score
- Cumulative EAE score/AUC
- Day of onset
- Proportion developing disease
- Frequency of Th17 cells in CNS or lymph nodes

If EAE clinical score is the primary endpoint, power around that. Treat microbiome and flow as secondary/exploratory unless powered separately.

**Essential.**

---

## B. Repeated measures ANOVA may not be ideal for EAE scores

Daily EAE clinical scores are:

- Ordinal
- Bounded
- Non-normal
- Longitudinal
- Autocorrelated
- Often zero-inflated before disease onset
- Vulnerable to missingness due to humane euthanasia

Repeated measures ANOVA assumes approximately continuous normally distributed outcomes and certain covariance structures. It may be too simplistic.

### Better options

Pre-specify one or more summary clinical endpoints:

- Area under the curve, AUC
- Maximum score
- Day of onset
- Disease incidence
- Mean score during peak window
- Mean score during chronic phase

Then analyze using appropriate models:

- Linear model or nonparametric test for AUC/peak score
- Survival analysis for disease onset
- Logistic regression/Fisher’s exact test for incidence
- Mixed-effects model for daily scores

For daily scores, consider:

- Mixed-effects ordinal regression, if available
- Linear mixed model as an approximation, with caution
- Generalized estimating equations
- Permutation-based approaches

Include random effects or blocking terms for:

- Cage
- Litter, if available
- Experimental batch

**Essential to revise analysis plan.**

---

## C. PERMANOVA has important caveats

PERMANOVA is common for microbiome beta diversity, but it can be misleading if groups differ in dispersion rather than centroid location.

### You should also test

- Homogeneity of dispersion, e.g., PERMDISP
- Alpha diversity
- Specific taxa or ASV differential abundance, with FDR correction
- Longitudinal trajectories

### Important

PERMANOVA should account for repeated measures and cage structure. Do not treat all fecal samples from all timepoints as independent.

You may need:

- Stratified permutations by mouse or cage
- Mixed models on distance-based ordinations
- Longitudinal microbiome models
- Separate analyses by timepoint with multiple-testing correction
- Models including baseline microbiome as covariate

**Essential.**

## D. Correlation of microbiome and immune parameters may be underpowered and non-causal

Correlating microbiome features with immune parameters is tempting but risky.

Problems:

- Many taxa create multiple-testing burden.
- Microbiome data are compositional.
- Cage creates non-independence.
- Disease severity may drive both microbiome and immune changes.
- n=12 per EAE group is small for correlation.
- Correlation does not prove microbiome-mediated mechanism.

### Recommendation

Treat microbiome-immune correlations as exploratory unless you have a specific pre-specified hypothesis, such as:

- Short-chain fatty acid-producing taxa correlate with Treg frequency.
- Fiber-induced taxa at day 7 predict EAE AUC.
- Butyrate-associated taxa correlate inversely with Th17 cells.

Also consider measuring functional metabolites directly.

**Important.**

High-fiber diets often act through microbial metabolites such as:

- Acetate
- Propionate

This would strengthen the mechanistic link considerably.
