### Critical Review of Experimental Design
---
#### 1. Strengths
Your design has several well-constructed elements that reduce bias and strengthen causal inference:
- Inclusion of both EAE and non-EAE control groups lets you disentangle diet effects specific to autoimmune disease from general effects of fiber on the microbiome/immune system, a major improvement over studies that only use EAE-treated mice.
- Baseline (day 0) fecal sample collection allows you to control for pre-existing microbiome differences between groups, a critical safeguard against confounding by pre-treatment microbiome variation.
- Blinding of clinical EAE scoring reduces risk of observer bias for your primary outcome.
- Repeated measurement of clinical EAE scores captures disease trajectory over time, rather than just a single endpoint, increasing sensitivity to detect treatment effects.
- Triangulation of complementary outcomes (clinical, microbiome, immune) lets you test whether diet effects on EAE align with your hypothesized microbiome-immune mechanism, rather than just reflecting a direct anti-inflammatory effect of fiber.
- Proactive consideration of cage and litter effects upfront, rather than ignoring these ubiquitous mouse study confounders.

---
#### 2. Missing Controls
Several critical controls are absent that are required to isolate the effect of your treatment and rule out alternative explanations:
- **Sham EAE control groups**: Your current "no EAE" groups receive no injections at all, but MOG-induced EAE requires administration of Complete Freund’s Adjuvant (CFA) and often pertussis toxin, both of which independently alter gut microbiome composition and immune function. You need two additional groups: (1) EAE-sham + standard diet, and (2) EAE-sham + high-fiber diet, which receive all induction injections except MOG peptide. Without these, you cannot determine if differences between EAE and non-EAE groups are due to autoimmune disease or the induction procedure itself.
- **Calorie intake control**: High-fiber diets are almost always lower in energy density, so mice on high-fiber diets often eat fewer total calories. Calorie restriction independently reduces EAE severity and alters the gut microbiome, so you need either (a) a pair-fed EAE control group (fed the same daily amount of standard diet as the high-fiber EAE group, measured per individual mouse) to isolate fiber-specific effects, or (b) individual daily food intake measurement for all mice with statistical adjustment for total calorie intake.
- **Blinding for non-clinical outcomes**: Only clinical scoring is blinded. 16S sequencing and flow cytometry analysis should also be performed by personnel blinded to group assignment to avoid unconscious bias in sample processing, gating, or taxonomic assignment.

---
#### 3. Potential Confounds
These factors could produce your expected results (fiber alters microbiome, reduces EAE) without your hypothesized mechanism (fiber → microbiome shift → reduced autoimmune inflammation):
- **Direct effects of fiber on immune function**: Fiber and its fermentation metabolites (e.g., short-chain fatty acids) can directly suppress T cell activation and inflammatory cytokine production, independent of microbiome changes. This would mean correlations between microbiome shifts and reduced T cell activation/EAE severity are spurious, as both are downstream of fiber rather than the microbiome driving immune changes.
- **Differential coprophagy**: Mice are obligate coprophagic, and high-fiber diets increase fecal output. If high-fiber mice consume more of their own feces, this could alter microbiome and immune outcomes independent of the administered diet itself.
- **Gut transit time changes**: Fiber alters gastrointestinal transit speed, which can change exposure of the gut immune system to MOG peptide/adjuvant during EAE induction, leading to differences in immune activation unrelated to microbiome composition.
- **Behavioral effects of diet**: If high-fiber mice are lethargic from gastrointestinal distress (e.g., bloating, diarrhea) during clinical scoring, this could inflate EAE severity scores independent of autoimmune neuroinflammation.
- **Estrous cycle variability**: Uncontrolled cyclic hormone fluctuations in female mice alter both EAE severity and gut microbiome composition, and could drive spurious group differences if not accounted for.

---
#### 4. Threats to Validity
##### Internal Validity (Could something other than treatment explain results?)
- Without sham EAE controls, you cannot isolate effects of autoimmune disease from effects of CFA/pertussis toxin.
- Without calorie intake control, diet effects could be fully explained by reduced energy consumption rather than fiber itself.
- Unaccounted cage/litter effects (you only have 2 cages per group, so you cannot estimate cage-level variance) could drive group differences if, for example, one cage per group has a subclinical infection or microclimate differences (light, temperature, airflow).
- Unblinded analysis of microbiome/immune outcomes introduces risk of detection bias.
- If day 0 fecal samples are collected after MOG induction (rather than pre-induction), pre-existing microbiome differences between groups could confound results.
- Uncontrolled estrous cycle fluctuations add unmeasured noise that can mimic or mask treatment effects.

##### External Validity (How generalizable are findings?)
- Findings are limited to young adult female C57BL/6 mice with acute, induced EAE, and may not translate to chronic relapsing-remitting autoimmune disease (the more common human MS phenotype) or to male mice, older mice, or other mouse strains.
- Effects will be specific to the exact fiber type and macronutrient composition of your high-fiber diet (e.g., inulin vs. cellulose vs. mixed fiber) and will not generalize to all "high-fiber" dietary patterns.
- 16S sequencing only captures bacterial taxa, so you will miss potential contributions of gut fungi, viruses, or archaea to microbiome-immune interactions.
- Immune sampling only at peak EAE (day 21) limits generalizability to other disease stages (e.g., remission, chronic progressive disease).

---
#### 5. Statistical Concerns
- **Sample size adequacy**: n=12 per EAE group is likely underpowered for PERMANOVA (a low-power test for microbiome differences, especially when adjusting for covariates like cage, litter, and baseline microbiome) and for correlation/mediation analysis (testing whether microbiome changes mediate diet effects on EAE requires far larger samples than testing main effects). n=6 per non-EAE group is very small for comparing microbiome/immune outcomes between diet groups in healthy mice, and will have low power to detect even large effects.
- **Mismatch between design and analysis**:
  - Clinical EAE scores are ordinal (0–5 scale), but repeated measures ANOVA assumes continuous, normally distributed data. This will violate model assumptions and reduce power. You need a mixed-effects model for ordinal data (e.g., proportional odds mixed model) that also handles missing data (e.g., mice euthanized before study end), which ANOVA cannot do.
  - Your planned analysis does not include mediation analysis to test your core mechanistic hypothesis (that microbiome changes mediate diet effects on EAE/immune outcomes). Simple correlation between compositional microbiome data and immune parameters is biased without compositional data transformation (see below).
  - You do not mention accounting for cage, litter, estrous cycle, or calorie intake as covariates/random effects in your planned analysis, even though you identified these as confounders. Failing to adjust for these will bias all group comparisons.
- **Compositional microbiome bias**: 16S count data are compositional (taxa sum to 1, creating spurious negative correlations between taxa). Correlating raw 16S counts with immune parameters will produce biased, uninterpretable results unless you apply centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation or compositional data analysis methods (e.g., ALDEx2) first.
- **Multiple testing burden**: If you test correlations between dozens of microbiome taxa and multiple immune populations, you will have a very high false positive rate unless you apply false discovery rate (FDR) or other multiple testing correction.

---
#### 6. Practical Issues
- **EAE induction failure**: MOG-induced EAE has variable penetrance, especially across different MOG/adjuvant batches. If a subset of mice fail to develop EAE, excluding them will reduce power, and uneven exclusion across groups will introduce bias. You will need a pre-specified plan for handling non-responders (e.g., include all mice in intention-to-treat analysis, or define a minimum clinical score threshold for EAE confirmation).
- **Diet acceptance**: Some mice refuse novel high-fiber diets, leading to uneven treatment exposure across groups. You will need to monitor individual food intake for the first 1–2 weeks of diet exposure to identify and address non-responders.
- **Fecal sample contamination**: High-fiber diets increase fecal output, raising risk of urine or bedding contamination of samples, which will alter 16S results. You will need a standardized, validated fecal collection protocol (e.g., immediate freezing at -80°C after collection) to minimize this.
- **Batch effects**: 16S sequencing and flow cytometry are highly sensitive to batch effects (e.g., different DNA extraction kits, antibody lots, staining times). If samples are not processed in batches that include all treatment groups, batch effects will swamp treatment effects.
- **Scoring bias risk**: Even with blinded clinical scoring, if high-fiber mice have distinct behavior (e.g., increased lethargy from digestion) or fecal appearance, scorers may be able to deduce group assignment, introducing unconscious bias. You will need to standardize housing and handling to minimize visible differences between groups.
- **Coprophagy control**: There is no feasible way to eliminate coprophagy in mice, so differential coprophagy between diet groups will be an uncontrolable confound unless you account for it in analysis (e.g., by measuring fecal output per mouse as a covariate).

---
#### 7. Recommendations
##### Essential Fixes (Must implement before starting; failure to do so will make results uninterpretable or highly biased)
1. Add sham EAE control groups (n=6–12 per group, matched to EAE groups for litter/cage assignment) that receive all induction injections except MOG, on both standard and high-fiber diets. This is non-negotiable to isolate effects of autoimmune disease from adjuvant effects.
2. Control for calorie intake: Either add a pair-fed EAE + standard diet group (fed the same daily amount as the high-fiber EAE group, measured per individual mouse) or measure individual daily food intake for all mice and include total calories as a covariate in all models.
3. Increase cage number to 3–4 cages per group (with 3–4 mice per cage to maintain total sample size) so you can include cage and litter as random effects in mixed models. With only 2 cages per group, you cannot estimate cage-level variance, so cage effects cannot be statistically controlled.
4. Replace repeated measures ANOVA with a mixed-effects model for ordinal EAE scores (e.g., proportional odds mixed model) that handles missing data and accounts for within-mouse correlation of repeated scores.
5. Apply centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation to all 16S count data before any correlation or group comparison, to eliminate compositional bias. Also include baseline (day 0) microbiome composition as a covariate in all microbiome analyses.
6. Track estrous cycle status (via vaginal cytology) for all female mice at baseline and key timepoints, and include estrous stage as a covariate in all models, or synchronize cycles across groups at study start.
7. Measure and report individual daily food intake for all mice, even if you do not pair-feed, to rule out calorie intake as an explanation for diet effects.

##### Nice-to-Haves (Improve rigor but are not required for basic validity)
1. Add a positive EAE treatment control group (e.g., EAE + standard diet + low-dose anti-IL-17) to confirm your EAE model is sensitive to therapeutic intervention in your facility, increasing confidence that null results for fiber are not due to model failure.
2. Collect baseline immune samples (e.g., mesenteric lymph nodes, spleen) at day 0 (pre-MOG induction) to distinguish diet effects on immune trajectories from endpoint differences.
3. Perform inter-rater reliability testing for clinical EAE scoring (calculate interclass correlation coefficient between two independent scorers for a subset of mice) to minimize observer bias.
4. Include mock microbial communities and negative DNA extraction controls in 16S sequencing to correct for contamination and sequencing error.
5. Process all 16S and flow cytometry samples in batches that include all treatment groups, and include batch as a covariate in analysis if batch effects are detected.
6. Collect fecal samples at a post-peak EAE timepoint (e.g., day 28) to assess whether microbiome changes persist after clinical recovery, improving generalizability to chronic disease stages.
7. If resources allow, use shotgun metagenomics instead of 16S to capture functional microbiome changes and non-bacterial microbes (fungi, viruses) that may contribute to microbiome-immune interactions.

---
#### Clarifying Questions (To enable more targeted feedback)
1. What is the exact macronutrient and fiber composition of your standard and high-fiber diets (e.g., fiber type, calorie density, protein/fat/carbohydrate content)?
2. When are MOG/CFA/pertussis toxin administered relative to your day 0 baseline fecal sample? Is day 0 pre- or post-induction?
3. Do your "no EAE" control groups receive sham injections (adjuvant only) or no injections at all?
4. How is clinical EAE scoring performed (e.g., 0–5 scale, specific criteria for each score)?
5. Is randomization stratified by baseline weight, litter, or estrous cycle to ensure balanced groups at baseline?
6. What effect size was your sample size calculation based on, and for which outcome?
7. Are mice housed 2 per cage, or more? What is the specific pathogen free status of your facility, and what is the light/dark cycle?
