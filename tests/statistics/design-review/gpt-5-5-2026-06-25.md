# 2. Missing controls

## Essential controls to consider

### 1. Sham-immunized controls, not just “No EAE” controls

Your current healthy controls are useful, but they may not control for the full EAE induction procedure.

MOG-EAE induction usually involves:

- MOG peptide
- Complete Freund’s Adjuvant, CFA
- Mycobacterium tuberculosis in CFA
- Pertussis toxin

These components can independently affect systemic inflammation, gut permeability, microbiome composition, and immune activation.

A better control would be something like:

- CFA + pertussis toxin without MOG peptide  
or  
- irrelevant peptide + CFA + pertussis toxin

This would help distinguish effects due to autoimmune antigen-specific disease from effects due to immunization/adjuvant-induced inflammation.

Possible revised controls:

| Group | Purpose |
|---|---|
| EAE + standard diet | disease control |
| EAE + high-fiber diet | main treatment |
| Sham/CFA/PTX + standard diet | immunization/adjuvant control |
| Sham/CFA/PTX + high-fiber diet | diet effect under inflammatory challenge |
| Naive + standard diet | baseline healthy control |
| Naive + high-fiber diet | baseline diet effect |

If resources are limited, I would prioritize sham/CFA/PTX controls over naive controls.

---

### 2. Diet-matched control diet

You need to be very careful about the “standard diet” versus “high-fiber diet.”

If the high-fiber diet differs from the standard diet in many ways, then any observed effect may not be due specifically to fiber.

Important diet variables:

- Total calories
- Fat content
- Protein content
- Simple carbohydrate content
- Micronutrients
- Phytoestrogens
- Source of fiber
- Soluble versus insoluble fiber
- Fermentability
- Pellet hardness
- Palatability
- Moisture content

Essential fix: use purified, matched diets where the primary difference is the fiber component.

For example:

- Control purified diet with cellulose or low fermentable fiber
- High-fiber diet matched for macronutrients and calories, differing mainly in fermentable fiber content

Also define the fiber type clearly. “High fiber” is not one intervention. Inulin, pectin, resistant starch, cellulose, psyllium, and mixed fibers can have very different microbiome and immune effects.

---

### 3. Baseline microbiome control before diet exposure

You list fecal samples at day 0, but it is important to define what “day 0” means.

Is day 0:

- Before diet begins?
- Before EAE induction?
- Both?
- After several weeks on diet?

If mice are assigned to diets at weaning and EAE is induced at 8 weeks, then by “day 0” the microbiome may already be strongly altered by diet. That can be fine, but you should collect a true baseline before diet exposure if possible.

Ideal microbiome sampling:

1. Before diet assignment  
2. Before EAE induction  
3. Early disease/preclinical phase  
4. Peak disease  
5. Chronic/recovery phase  

This allows you to ask whether diet-induced microbiome changes existed before disease onset.

---

### 4. Pair-feeding or food intake control

High-fiber diets can change food intake, caloric absorption, body weight, stool output, and energy balance. If high-fiber mice eat less or absorb fewer calories, reduced EAE severity could be due to altered metabolism rather than fiber-specific microbiome effects.

At minimum:

- Measure food intake per cage
- Measure body weight frequently
- Consider energy density of diets
- Consider pair-feeding if intake differences are substantial

Pair-feeding is not always necessary, but food intake and body weight monitoring are essential.

---

### 5. Vehicle/procedure controls for microbiome sampling and handling

Handling frequency, cage disturbance, fecal collection time, and stress can affect microbiome and immune outcomes.

Standardize:

- Time of day of fecal collection
- Collection method
- Storage time before freezing
- Freezing temperature
- DNA extraction batch
- Sequencing run
- Cage changing schedule

These are not “groups” but are important procedural controls.

# 3. Potential confounds

These are factors that could produce the expected result — high-fiber diet changes microbiome and reduces EAE — without necessarily supporting your hypothesized microbiome-immune mechanism.

## A. Cage effects

This is probably the biggest issue.

You mention 2 cages per group. That is a major concern.

For microbiome studies, mice in the same cage are not independent because they share:

- Microbes
- Bedding
- Feces/coprophagy
- Microenvironment
- Stress conditions
- Food hopper
- Water bottle

If each diet group has only 2 cages, then diet effects may be partially or completely confounded with cage effects.

For example, if one high-fiber cage randomly starts with a microbiome that protects against EAE, it may look like a diet effect.

This creates pseudoreplication if you analyze each mouse as an independent microbiome sample without accounting for cage.

Essential fix: increase the number of cages per group and include cage as a random effect or blocking factor.

For microbiome outcomes, 4–6 cages per group is much better than 2 cages per group, even if total mouse number stays similar.

---

## B. Litter effects

Mice from the same litter share maternal microbiota, early-life environment, genetics, and immune development. If litter is unevenly distributed across groups, litter could masquerade as diet effect.

Example problem:

- Litter A mostly assigned to high-fiber
- Litter B mostly assigned to standard diet

Then differences may reflect maternal/litter microbiome rather than diet.

Essential fix:

- Randomize within litter
- Distribute littermates across treatment groups
- Track litter ID
- Include litter as a random effect if feasible

However, avoid putting all littermates from one treatment in the same cage, or litter and cage become inseparable.

---

## C. Timing of diet exposure

You assign mice to groups at weaning, but EAE is induced at 8 weeks.

That means the diet acts during immune development from weaning to adulthood. If high fiber reduces EAE, it may be due to developmental immune programming rather than an effect during active autoimmune inflammation.

That may be scientifically interesting, but it changes interpretation.

You need to decide whether your question is:

1. Does lifelong/post-weaning fiber exposure alter susceptibility to EAE?  
or  
2. Does fiber supplementation at/after EAE induction reduce disease?  
or  
3. Does fiber therapeutically reduce established EAE?

These are different questions.

Possible design options:

- Preventive design: start diet before EAE induction
- Concurrent design: start diet at EAE induction
- Therapeutic design: start diet after clinical onset

Your current design is preventive/developmental, not therapeutic.

---

## D. Diet palatability and caloric intake

High-fiber diets may reduce energy intake, alter weight gain, or change metabolic state. Caloric restriction itself can reduce inflammatory disease severity.

If high-fiber mice have lower body weight or altered leptin/insulin/metabolic status, the effect may not be microbiome-mediated.

Measure:

- Food intake
- Body weight
- Fecal output
- Possibly serum metabolites or SCFAs

---

## E. Disease severity can alter microbiome

EAE itself can change feeding behavior, mobility, stress hormones, gut motility, and microbiome composition.

Therefore, if high-fiber mice have milder disease and different microbiomes at day 14 or 21, it may not mean the microbiome caused the reduced disease. The altered microbiome may be a consequence of less severe disease.

To strengthen causal inference, focus on microbiome changes before clinical onset.

Important:

- Analyze preclinical fecal samples separately.
- Ask whether early microbiome changes predict later disease severity.

---

## F. Batch effects

Potential batch effects include:

- EAE induction day
- MOG peptide batch
- CFA batch
- Pertussis toxin batch
- Person performing immunization
- Flow cytometry staining batch
- Sequencing batch
- DNA extraction batch
- Cage rack position
- Room/shelf effects

If all high-fiber mice are processed separately from standard-diet mice, batch effects can mimic treatment effects.

Essential fix:

- Randomize sample processing order
- Balance groups across extraction batches, sequencing runs, flow panels, and EAE induction batches
- Record batch variables

---

## G. Sex-specific effects

Using only female mice is common in EAE because disease incidence can be robust, and autoimmune disease is often female-biased. But it limits generalizability.

Also, female mice can vary with estrous cycle, although for EAE this is usually less problematic than cage/litter effects.

External validity issue: findings may not apply to males.

---

## H. Microbiome vendor/facility specificity

Microbiome-dependent effects often vary dramatically by vendor and animal facility.

A high-fiber diet may protect mice from one vendor but not another due to baseline microbial differences.

This is a major external validity limitation.

# 4. Threats to validity

## Internal validity: could something other than treatment explain results?

Yes. Main internal validity threats:

### 1. Cage-level confounding
With only 2 cages per group, cage effects could drive microbiome and even immune differences.

### 2. Litter imbalance
If litters are not balanced across groups, early-life microbiome differences could explain results.

### 3. Non-equivalent diets
If diets differ in multiple components, the effect cannot be attributed specifically to fiber.

### 4. Food intake/body weight differences
Reduced disease could reflect altered caloric intake or body weight rather than microbiome-mediated immune modulation.

### 5. Adjuvant/immunization effects
Without sham-immunized controls, you cannot cleanly separate antigen-specific autoimmunity from CFA/PTX-driven inflammation.

### 6. Reverse causality in microbiome data
Disease severity can alter the microbiome, so microbiome differences after disease onset do not necessarily cause disease differences.

### 7. Batch effects
Sequencing or flow cytometry batch effects can produce apparent group differences.

---

## External validity: how generalizable are findings?

Potential limitations:

### 1. Single mouse strain
C57BL/6 is standard but genetically narrow. Effects may differ in other strains.

### 2. Female-only design
Findings may not apply to males.

### 3. One EAE model
MOG-induced active EAE in C57BL/6 mice models certain aspects of autoimmune CNS inflammation but not all aspects of human multiple sclerosis.

### 4. Facility-specific microbiome
Microbiome findings may be highly institution-specific.

### 5. Specific fiber type
If you use one fiber source, conclusions apply to that fiber, not necessarily to “high fiber” broadly.

### 6. Preventive rather than therapeutic design
If diet starts at weaning, conclusions apply to early/preventive exposure, not necessarily treatment after autoimmune disease develops.

# 5. Statistical concerns

## A. Sample size may be inadequate for microbiome/cage-level inference

You have n=12 EAE mice per diet group, which may be reasonable for clinical EAE scoring if your lab has strong prior data.

However, for microbiome outcomes, the effective sample size is closer to the number of cages than the number of mice.

With 2 cages per group, you have very limited ability to distinguish cage effects from diet effects.

This is a major concern.

Better:

- Increase to at least 4 cages per group, ideally 5–6.
- Keep fewer mice per cage if necessary.
- Treat cage as a random effect or include cage/blocking in analysis.

Example:

Instead of 2 cages × 6 mice per group, use:

- 4 cages × 3 mice per group  
or  
- 6 cages × 2 mice per group  

The second option may be better for microbiome independence, though practical housing constraints matter.

---

## B. Repeated-measures ANOVA may not be appropriate for EAE scores

Clinical EAE scores are:

- Ordinal, not continuous
- Often non-normal
- Zero-inflated early in disease
- Longitudinal/repeated
- Sometimes missing due to humane euthanasia
- Nonlinear over time

Repeated-measures ANOVA assumes properties that EAE scores often violate.

Better options:

### Primary clinical endpoints
Predefine one or more primary EAE endpoints:

- Incidence
- Day of onset
- Maximum score
- Cumulative disease index / area under the curve
- Mean score during peak window
- Score at day 21
- Weight loss

For repeated daily scores:

- Mixed-effects model with mouse as repeated unit and cage as random effect
- Ordinal mixed model if feasible
- Nonparametric or permutation methods for AUC/max score
- Survival analysis for disease onset

Repeated-measures ANOVA is not fatal, but it is not ideal.

---

## C. PERMANOVA needs careful design

PERMANOVA is commonly used for microbiome beta diversity, but there are caveats.

### Problems:
PERMANOVA can detect differences in group dispersion, not just group centroid differences. So if high-fiber mice have more variable microbiomes, PERMANOVA may be significant even without a consistent compositional shift.

You should also test:

- Homogeneity of dispersion, e.g. PERMDISP/betadisper
- Alpha diversity
- Specific taxa or ASVs with appropriate multiple-testing correction
- Longitudinal changes using models that account for repeated measures

### Repeated microbiome sampling
Fecal samples from the same mouse over time are not independent.

Your analysis should account for:

- Mouse ID
- Cage
- Timepoint
- Diet
- EAE status
- Diet × EAE interaction
- Diet × time interaction
- EAE × time interaction

A simple PERMANOVA comparing groups at each timepoint may be acceptable as exploratory, but not sufficient as the main longitudinal analysis.

---

## D. Correlation between microbiome and immune parameters is risky

Correlations between microbiome features and immune measures are useful for hypothesis generation but do not prove causality.

Also, microbiome data are high-dimensional and compositional. Naive correlations can be misleading.

Concerns:

- Multiple comparisons
- Compositionality
- Cage confounding
- Disease severity as a third variable
- Time mismatch between fecal microbiome and immune endpoint

Better:

- Predefine taxa/metabolites of interest if possible
- Use appropriate compositional methods
- Adjust for cage and disease severity
- Correct for multiple testing
- Treat correlation as exploratory unless you add causal experiments

---

## E. Need to define primary endpoint

You currently list multiple outcomes:

- Clinical score
- 16S microbiome composition
- Flow cytometry T cell populations
- Correlations

That is fine, but you should define one primary outcome before starting.

Possible primary outcome:

> Cumulative EAE disease burden from day 0 to day 21 in EAE high-fiber versus EAE standard diet.

Secondary outcomes:

- Day of onset
- Maximum score
- Body weight loss
- Microbiome beta diversity
- SCFA-producing taxa
- Treg/Th17 ratio
- CNS-infiltrating CD4 T cell subsets

This avoids ambiguity after data collection.

# 6. Practical issues: what could go wrong?

## A. EAE incidence or severity may be too low or too variable
EAE can vary by batch, peptide, CFA, pertussis toxin, sex, vendor, season, and technician.

If standard diet controls do not develop robust disease, you may not be able to detect protection.

Plan:

- Use a validated EAE induction protocol
- Include positive historical benchmarks
- Randomize induction order
- Use same reagent batches when possible
- Monitor body weight and humane endpoints

---

## B. High-fiber diet may reduce food intake
Mice may dislike the diet or eat less. This can cause weight differences and confound disease severity.

Monitor per-cage consumption and body weight from the start.

---

## C. Diet may need time to alter microbiome
If diet starts too close to EAE induction, microbiome changes may not occur before immune priming. If diet starts at weaning, interpretation shifts toward developmental/preventive effects.

Be explicit about your intended biological window.

---

## D. Fecal sample quality and timing
Microbiome results are sensitive to sample handling.

Problems include:

- Samples warming before freezing
- Variable collection times
- Contamination
- Different extraction batches by group
- Low biomass issues less likely with feces, but still relevant

Standardize and randomize processing.

---

## E. Flow cytometry endpoint may miss relevant biology
Day 21 may be too late or too early depending on disease kinetics.

In MOG-EAE, relevant immune events occur in phases:

- Priming phase: draining lymph nodes/spleen before clinical onset
- Peak disease: CNS infiltration and effector function
- Chronic/recovery phase: regulation/remission

A single day-21 immune endpoint may miss key differences.

Consider adding:

- Preclinical/priming timepoint, e.g. day 7–10
- Peak disease timepoint, e.g. day 14–18 depending on your model
- Day 21 for chronic/resolution phase

If resources are limited, choose timepoints based on expected peak disease in your facility.

---

## F. Clinical scoring blinding may be compromised
Diet pellets or cage labels may reveal group assignment.

Make sure the scorer cannot see diet type or cage labeling that indicates group.

---

## G. Mortality/euthanasia can bias longitudinal analysis
If severe mice are euthanized, later scores are missing non-randomly. You need a predefined rule for handling post-euthanasia scores in analysis.

Options include:

- Assign maximum score after euthanasia for disease-burden analysis
- Use survival/time-to-event analysis
- Predefine humane endpoint handling
