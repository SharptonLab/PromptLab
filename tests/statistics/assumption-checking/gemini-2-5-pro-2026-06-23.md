### 1. List of PERMANOVA Assumptions (in order of importance)

1.  **Independence of Samples:** This is the most critical assumption. Violating it completely invalidates the p-values. It is an assumption about your study design, not your data.

### 2. Detailed Assumption Checks

#### **Assumption 1: Independence of Samples**

*   **What it means:** Each sample (e.g., each fecal pellet, soil core, or water sample) was collected independently of all other samples. The measurement from one sample does not provide any information or influence on the measurement of another. Common violations include taking multiple samples from the same subject over time (repeated measures) or from the same cage (clustering) and treating them as independent replicates.
*   **How to test it:** This is not tested with a statistical test but by **critically evaluating your experimental design**.
    *   Are any samples technical replicates? They should be averaged or handled appropriately before analysis.
    *   Are samples from the same individual, site, or cage? If so, they are not independent.
*   **How to interpret the results:** If samples are not independent, you have "pseudoreplication," which artificially inflates your statistical power and leads to an unacceptably high rate of false positives.
*   **What to do if violated:** You must account for the non-independence in your model. In `vegan::adonis2`, you can do this by defining a `strata` argument, which constrains permutations to occur only *within* a specified blocking factor (e.g., `SubjectID`). This is the correct way to handle repeated measures or nested designs.

#### **Assumption 2: Homogeneity of Multivariate Dispersion**

*   **What it means:** The "spread" or variability of the samples within each group is roughly equal. In a PCoA plot, this means the cloud of points for each group should occupy a similarly sized area. It is the multivariate analogue of homogeneity of variances (Levene's test) in a standard ANOVA.
*   **Why it's critical:** PERMANOVA tests for differences in the *location* (centroid) and/or *dispersion* of the groups.
    *   If dispersion is equal, a significant p-value means the group centroids are different (the result you are likely looking for).
    *   If dispersion is unequal, a significant p-value could be due to different centroids, different dispersions, or both. You cannot distinguish these effects with the PERMANOVA result alone.
*   **How to test it:**

*   **How to interpret the results:** The `permutest()` on the `betadisper` object will give you a p-value.
    *   **p > 0.05:** The assumption is met. The groups have similar dispersion. You can proceed and interpret a significant PERMANOVA result as a difference in community composition (centroids).
