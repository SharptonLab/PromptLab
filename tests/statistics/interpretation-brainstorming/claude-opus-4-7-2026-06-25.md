Research question: Does fecal microbiota transplantation from lean donors improve metabolic outcomes in obese mice?

# Critical Analysis of Your FMT Experiment

## 1. Support for Your Initial Interpretation

**Reasonably strong elements:**
- **Dose-response-like pattern**: Lean FMT > obese FMT > control on glucose, with lean being significant. This monotonic ordering is consistent with donor microbiota carrying transferable metabolic effects.
- **Engraftment confirmed**: Donor-derived taxa detected in recipients—rules out the trivial possibility that nothing actually transferred.
- **Mechanistic plausibility**: Microbiome composition differs between groups, providing a candidate mediator.
- **Weight-independent effect**: Glucose improvement without weight change is consistent with prior literature suggesting microbiota affects glucose metabolism via mechanisms (bile acids, SCFAs, incretins) distinct from adiposity.

**Weaknesses:**
- You have **one experiment, one donor pool** (presumably). The effect could be specific to these donors, not "lean donors" generally.
- The glucose effect (145 vs 180 mg/dL) is at a single timepoint; you don't have insulin, HOMA-IR, or glucose tolerance curves to characterize the metabolic phenotype.
- p=0.01 and p=0.03 with n=10 are not robust—these are vulnerable to outliers and would not survive aggressive multiple-comparison correction across all your measurements.

## 2. Alternative Interpretations

**A. Non-specific FMT effect (any microbiota perturbation helps)**
- *Prediction*: Both FMT groups should show some improvement vs control. Your data partially support this—FMT-obese showed a non-significant trend toward lower glucose.
- *Distinguishing test*: Include an "autologous FMT" or heat-killed FMT control. If those also improve glucose, the effect isn't lean-donor-specific.

**B. Diversity per se, not specific taxa**
- *Prediction*: Higher α-diversity correlates with lower glucose regardless of composition.
- *Distinguishing test*: Within-group correlation between individual diversity and glucose; compare to correlation with specific taxon abundance.

**C. Donor confound (donor effect, not "lean" effect)**
- If your "lean donors" differ from "obese donors" in age, sex, cage, vendor, diet history, or genetic background, you're measuring those differences, not leanness.
- *Distinguishing test*: Multiple independent donor pools per phenotype; ideally co-housed donors fed different diets to produce lean/obese phenotypes from matched backgrounds.

**D. Vehicle/handling artifact**
- PBS control may not adequately control for the FMT procedure (gavage volume, fecal matrix components, dead bacteria, metabolites in donor stool independent of live bacteria).
- *Distinguishing test*: Autoclaved/filtered FMT control would isolate live-microbiota effects from metabolite effects.

**E. Cage effects**
- If mice within a group were co-housed, they share microbiota via coprophagy. Your effective n may be closer to number of cages than number of mice—a major issue in microbiome studies.
- *Distinguishing test*: Reanalyze with cage as random effect; report number of cages.

**F. Regression to the mean / baseline imbalance**
- Were baseline fasting glucose levels equivalent across groups before treatment? If FMT-lean started lower, you're seeing baseline difference, not treatment effect.

**G. Stress/behavior mediation**
- FMT procedures, microbiome changes, or specific taxa can alter feeding behavior, activity, or stress. Reduced food intake could lower glucose without weight change being significant at n=10.
- *Distinguishing test*: Food intake and activity data.

## 3. Potential Confounds

- **Cage effects** (above)—the single biggest unaddressed issue in many FMT studies
- **Coprophagy across groups** if housed in same room with shared air/equipment
- **Batch effects** in glucose measurement (when were samples run? blinded?)
- **Fasting duration variability**—fasting glucose is highly sensitive to exact fast length
- **Circadian timing** of measurements
- **Antibiotic pretreatment** (if used)—incomplete depletion creates variable engraftment
- **Donor stool processing**: anaerobic vs aerobic prep can shift which taxa survive
- **Diet continuation**: were all groups on identical HFD throughout? Any diet differences swamp microbiome effects

## 4. Limitations to Acknowledge

1. Single donor pool per condition limits generalizability to "lean donors" as a class
2. Single timepoint glucose measurement is a weak metabolic phenotype
3. n=10 mice (and likely fewer cages) limits statistical power and reliability
4. No mechanistic data linking specific taxa to glucose phenotype—correlation only
5. Mouse model translatability to human obesity is limited
6. 8 weeks may not capture durability of effect
7. No assessment of insulin sensitivity, beta-cell function, or hepatic glucose production—you don't know *where* glucose homeostasis improved
8. Multiple comparisons across microbiome features not corrected (presumably)

## 5. Follow-up Experiments

**To strengthen causality:**
- GTT/ITT for proper metabolic phenotyping
- Insulin, GLP-1, bile acids, SCFAs as candidate mediators
- Multiple independent donor pools

**To test mechanism:**
- Identify candidate taxa via differential abundance + correlation with glucose
- Mono-colonization or defined consortium of candidates in gnotobiotic mice
- Metabolomics of cecal contents

**To test specificity:**
- Autologous FMT and/or heat-killed FMT controls
- Lean donors on HFD vs obese donors on chow—decouple phenotype from diet history
- Time course to assess effect onset and durability

**Methodological:**
- Cage-level analysis
- Pre-registration of follow-up

## 6. What the Results Don't Show (Overreach to Avoid)

- ❌ "Lean microbiota cures metabolic disease"—you have one glucose measurement, not metabolic disease
- ❌ "Specific bacterial taxa improve glucose"—you've shown correlation, not causation, for any taxon
- ❌ "FMT could treat type 2 diabetes in humans"—mouse model, short term, no insulin data
- ❌ "Lean-donor effect is reproducible"—single donor pool
- ❌ "The microbiome mediates the effect"—you haven't shown mediation, only that microbiome changed *and* glucose changed in the same group
- ❌ "Weight-independent effect"—possibly underpowered to detect weight differences; report confidence intervals on weight, don't claim null
- ❌ Mechanistic claims about bile acids, SCFAs, inflammation, etc., without measuring them

**A reasonable framing**: "FMT from lean donors was associated with improved fasting glucose in HFD-fed mice, independent of detectable weight changes, with concurrent shifts in microbiome composition. These findings are consistent with—but do not establish—a causal role for transferred microbiota in glucose regulation. Mechanistic and donor-replication studies are needed."

## One Question Worth Asking Yourself

The FMT-obese group showed a non-significant trend toward lower glucose. Is this noise, or is there a real "any FMT" effect that you're partially attributing to "lean FMT" specifically? With n=10, you may not be able to distinguish these, and this is exactly the kind of thing a reviewer will press on. Be honest about it in the manuscript rather than dismissing the trend.
