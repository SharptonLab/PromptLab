## Part 1: Community-Level Analysis (Beta Diversity)

### Approach 1: PERMANOVA (Permutational Multivariate ANOVA)

1. **Method**: PERMANOVA via `adonis2()` in R's vegan package, applied to your Bray-Curtis distance matrix.
2. **When appropriate**: Testing whether group centroids differ in multivariate space using a distance matrix; the standard first-line test for beta diversity differences between groups.
3. **Key assumptions**:
   - Independent observations (✓ you have this)
   - **Homogeneity of multivariate dispersions** between groups (test with `betadisper()` + `permutest()`)
   - Exchangeability under the null
4. **Advantages**:
   - Distribution-free (permutation-based)
   - Handles high-dimensional data with small n
   - Can adjust for confounders (age, sex, stage, prior treatment) by including them in the formula
   - Provides an R² (effect size)
5. **Limitations**:
   - A significant result can reflect either location OR dispersion differences—must check `betadisper`
   - Order of terms in the formula matters (use marginal tests: `by = "margin"`)
   - With n=45 and 4+ confounders, you have limited power; consider parsimony
6. **Alternatives**: ANOSIM (less flexible, no covariate adjustment—not recommended when PERMANOVA is feasible); MRPP.

### Approach 2: Constrained Ordination (db-RDA / CAP)

1. **Method**: Distance-based redundancy analysis (`dbrda()` or `capscale()` in vegan).
2. **When appropriate**: When you want to both *test* and *visualize* the variance in community composition explained by response status while controlling for covariates.
3. **Key assumptions**: Similar to PERMANOVA; appropriate distance metric chosen.
4. **Advantages**: Produces an interpretable ordination conditioned on covariates; permutation tests for significance; complements PERMANOVA.
5. **Limitations**: Visualization can be over-interpreted; same dispersion caveats apply.
6. **Alternatives**: Unconstrained PCoA with group overlay (descriptive only, no inference).
