Context: I am preparing a response to reviewers for a manuscript on microbiome-metabolome interactions in inflammatory bowel disease.

Reviewer comment:
"The authors correlate microbiome composition with metabolite profiles and identify several significant associations. However, the analysis appears to suffer from multiple testing issues. The authors mention using FDR correction, but do not report how many tests were performed or what the effective correction threshold was. Additionally, some of the highlighted correlations have r-values below 0.3, which may not be biologically meaningful even if statistically significant. The authors should address the multiple testing concern more rigorously and discuss the biological relevance of weak correlations."

### Response to Reviewer Comment
We sincerely thank the reviewer for this critical and constructive feedback, which highlights essential considerations for rigorous interpretation of high-dimensional microbiome-metabolome association studies. We agree that transparent reporting of multiple testing procedures and careful discussion of the biological relevance of effect sizes (including weak correlations) are critical for validating our findings.

As the reviewer noted, our original manuscript did not explicitly report the total number of multiple comparisons performed, the FDR correction threshold, or the effective number of tests, and did not adequately address the limited biological interpretability of statistically significant correlations with *r* < 0.3. We have revised the manuscript to address both concerns as detailed below.

#### 1. Transparency of multiple testing procedures
We confirm that all pairwise correlations between microbial taxa and metabolite abundances were corrected for multiple testing using the Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) procedure, with a significance threshold of *q* < 0.05. A total of 847 independent tests were performed (77 taxa × 11 metabolites). We have added this information to the Statistical Analysis subsection of the Methods to improve reporting transparency. The revised text reads (Page 4, Lines 110–115):
> "To account for multiple comparisons across correlations between microbial taxa and measured metabolite abundances, we applied Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) correction to all pairwise tests, with a significance threshold of *q* < 0.05. A total of 847 independent tests were performed (77 taxa × 11 metabolites)."

#### 2. Interpretation of weak statistically significant correlations
We fully agree that correlations with *r* < 0.3, which explain less than 9% of shared variance between paired taxa and metabolites, should be interpreted with caution, as they may reflect indirect relationships, unmeasured confounding variables, or noise rather than direct, biologically meaningful associations. We have added explicit discussion of this limitation to the manuscript, and have retained these weak significant associations in our results and network analysis, as they contribute to identifying broader community-level interaction patterns that are not detectable when focusing solely on strong individual correlations. The revised Discussion text reads (Page 7, Lines 208–218):
> "We note that several statistically significant correlations identified in this study have *r*-values < 0.3, which explain limited shared variance between taxa and metabolite abundances. These weak associations should be interpreted with caution, as they may reflect indirect relationships or unmeasured confounding variables, rather than direct mechanistic links. However, these weak pairwise associations are retained in our network analysis, as they can capture indirect or context-dependent interactions that are biologically relevant at the community level, even if they do not reflect strong direct pairwise relationships. Future work validating these associations in independent cohorts and mechanistic models will be critical to confirm their biological relevance."

We hope these revisions adequately address the reviewer’s concerns. Thank you again for the thoughtful feedback that has strengthened the rigor and transparency of our manuscript.
