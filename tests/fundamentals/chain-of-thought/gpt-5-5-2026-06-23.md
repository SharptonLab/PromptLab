> Does the probiotic change microbiome community composition differently over time compared with placebo?

Statistically, that is primarily a **treatment × time interaction** question.

## Step 3 — Assumption evaluation for your specific study

### PERMANOVA concerns

PERMANOVA is attractive because it directly addresses community composition, but your design complicates it.

Potential problems:

1. **Repeated measures**  
   If you treat all 60 samples as independent, the p-values will be anticonservative because samples from the same mouse are correlated.

2. **Cage effects**  
   If cage is ignored, apparent treatment effects may actually reflect cage-level differences.

3. **Permutation scheme**  
   Standard PERMANOVA assumes samples are exchangeable. In your case, samples are not freely exchangeable because of mouse and cage structure.

4. **Treatment may be confounded with cage**  
   If all mice in a cage receive the same treatment, the effective number of independent treatment units may be the number of cages, not the number of mice.

5. **Small sample size**  
   With 20 mice and perhaps only 7–10 cages, complex models may have limited power.

6. **Dispersion differences**  
   PERMANOVA can be significant if probiotic mice are more variable than placebo mice, even if their average community composition is not shifted. You would need to check group dispersion, for example using `betadisper`.

Bottom line: PERMANOVA is useful, but only if the repeated-measures and cage structure are handled correctly.
