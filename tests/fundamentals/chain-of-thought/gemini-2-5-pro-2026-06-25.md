### **Step 3 - Assumption evaluation**

Let's evaluate how each approach handles your specific data structure.

*   **PERMANOVA:**
    *   **Strength:** It works directly on distance matrices, which elegantly sidesteps the compositional data problem and the normality assumption. It's the standard for testing overall community differences.

A standard PERMANOVA formula like `distance ~ Treatment * Time` violates the assumption of exchangeability for permutation. You must use a **constrained permutation scheme**, where you shuffle data only within each mouse to account for the repeated measures. Handling the additional cage effect within this framework is even more challenging and not straightforward in most standard software packages. The assumption of homogeneous dispersion must also be tested (e.g., with the `betadisper` function in R's `vegan` package).

*   **Linear Mixed-Effects Models (LMMs):**
    *   **Strength:** This is the *ideal framework* for your experimental design. You can explicitly model the Treatment x Time interaction (fixed effects) while simultaneously accounting for repeated measures and cage effects (as random effects). *   **GLMMs / Compositional Models:**
    *   **Strength:** These are arguably the most statistically rigorous and appropriate methods.
