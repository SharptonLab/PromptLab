# Microbiome Statistical Analysis: Step-by-Step Reasoning

## Step 1 — Data Structure

Your data has several features that strongly constrain analysis choices:

**Compositional nature (critical):** Relative abundances are constrained to sum to 1 (or 100%) per sample. This means abundances are *not independent* — if one taxon increases, others must decrease. Standard methods that assume independent variables (t-tests, ANOVA, standard regression) are technically inappropriate and can produce spurious associations. This is the "compositional data problem" (Aitchison; Gloor et al. 2017).

**High dimensionality:** You likely have hundreds of taxa (or ASVs/OTUs) but only 20 mice × 3 timepoints = 60 samples. p >> n, so multivariate methods need regularization or focus on community-level summaries.

**Sparsity and zero-inflation:** Many taxa will have zero counts in many samples. Zeros can be "structural" (taxon absent) or "sampling" (taxon present but undetected). This affects log-ratio transformations (log(0) is undefined).

**Repeated measures:** Each mouse contributes 3 timepoints — observations within a mouse are correlated. Independence is violated.

**Nested/hierarchical structure:** Mice are nested within cages (2–3 per cage). Cage effects are well-documented in microbiome studies — co-housed mice share microbiota through coprophagy. Ignoring this inflates Type I error and conflates treatment with cage.

**Small sample size:** n=10 per group is modest; cage clustering reduces effective sample size further. The effective number of independent units may be closer to your number of cages (~6–10 per treatment) than mice.

**Longitudinal question:** "Affects composition *over time*" implies you care about treatment × time interactions, not just overall differences.

## Step 2 — Candidate Approaches

**A. Community-level (multivariate) methods:**

1. **PERMANOVA** (`adonis2` in vegan): Tests whether group centroids in a distance space (e.g., Bray-Curtis, UniFrac, Aitchison) differ. Assumes exchangeability under the null. Can include strata/blocks for repeated measures.

2. **Constrained ordination (db-RDA, CCA):** Tests how much variance treatment explains while controlling for covariates.

3. **PERMANOVA with repeated-measures structure** or **mixed-effects on distance matrices** (e.g., `MicrobiomeStat`, `LDM`, `MiRKAT-S` for longitudinal).

**B. Taxon-by-taxon (univariate) differential abundance:**

4. **ANCOM-BC2**: Log-ratio-based, handles compositionality, allows random effects (cage, mouse) and repeated measures. Current field standard.

5. **MaAsLin2/3**: Linear mixed models on transformed (arcsine-sqrt or log) abundances with random effects for mouse and cage. Widely used, flexible covariate handling.

6. **LinDA, DESeq2, limma-voom, ALDEx2:** Other options; vary in compositional awareness and longitudinal support.

**C. Longitudinal-specific community methods:**

7. **SplinectomeR / MetaLonDA / MicrobiomeStat (LinDA-LM):** Designed for longitudinal differential abundance.

8. **PERMANOVA with time as factor + interaction**, restricting permutations within mouse.

## Step 3 — Assumption Evaluation

| Approach | Concerns for your data |
|---|---|
| **PERMANOVA (basic)** | Assumes independent observations — violated by repeated measures and cage clustering. Need to constrain permutations (`strata = mouse`, blocks for cage) or use repeated-measures variants. Also assumes similar within-group dispersions (check with `betadisper`). |
| **ANCOM-BC2** | Handles compositionality well; supports random effects. Main concern is power with n=20 and many taxa after FDR correction. |
| **MaAsLin2** | Mixed-model framework handles cage + mouse random effects elegantly. Compositionality only partially addressed by transformation. |
| **DESeq2/edgeR** | Designed for count data, not strictly compositional; can work but less defensible for compositional inference. |
| **Standard mixed models on raw proportions** | Violates compositionality and normality; not recommended. |

**Cross-cutting issue:** With only ~6–10 cages per arm, cage effects may be partially confounded with treatment. If all cages within a group received the same treatment (likely), you cannot fully separate "treatment effect" from "cage effect" — cage is the experimental unit for treatment, not the mouse. This is a design issue, not a statistical one, but it caps your effective n.

## Step 4 — Practical Considerations

- **Field conventions:** A typical microbiome paper reports (a) alpha diversity over time (mixed model), (b) beta diversity with PERMANOVA + ordination (PCoA/NMDS), (c) differential abundance with ANCOM-BC2 or MaAsLin2. Reviewers will expect this combination.
- **Software:** All recommended tools are R-based and well-maintained (vegan, ANCOMBC, Maaslin2, phyloseq, mia/miaverse).
- **Interpretability:** Community-level results ("composition differs") are easier to communicate; taxon-level results identify specific drivers but require multiple-testing correction.
- **Reproducibility:** Pre-register the analysis plan if possible; specify distance metric, transformation, and random-effects structure before looking at results.

## Step 5 — Recommendation

**Suggested analytical strategy to discuss with a statistician:**

1. **Alpha diversity (Shannon, Faith's PD):** Linear mixed model with fixed effects for treatment, time, treatment×time; random intercepts for mouse and cage.

2. **Beta diversity (community-level):** PERMANOVA on Aitchison distance (compositionally aware) or weighted UniFrac, with permutations constrained within cage/mouse. Include treatment, time, and interaction. Check dispersion with `betadisper`. Visualize with PCoA.

3. **Differential abundance (taxon-level):** ANCOM-BC2 or MaAsLin2 with random effects for mouse (repeated measures) and cage (clustering), fixed effects for treatment × time. Apply FDR correction.

4. **Sensitivity analyses:** Aggregate to genus level (more power, less sparsity); rerun without cage random effect to gauge its impact.

**Questions to ask your statistician:**

1. **Is cage or mouse the appropriate experimental unit for treatment effects given my design?** (This determines effective sample size and may change recommended methods.)
2. **How should I handle the confounding between cage and treatment?** Can cage be modeled as a random effect, or is it nested too tightly with treatment to identify?
3. **Which distance metric is most appropriate** given my biological question (Aitchison for compositional rigor, UniFrac if phylogeny matters, Bray-Curtis for convention)?
4. **For PERMANOVA with repeated measures, should I use `strata` in `adonis2`, a repeated-measures permutation scheme, or a different framework like LDM/MiRKAT-S?**
5. **Given n=20 and likely high zero-inflation, what is my realistic power to detect taxon-level effects after FDR correction?** Should I prefilter low-prevalence taxa (e.g., present in <10% of samples)?
6. **Should I model time as continuous (3 levels is borderline) or categorical?**
7. **Are there any design changes I should make for the next experiment** (e.g., more cages with fewer mice per cage to increase the effective n for treatment)?

The last question is worth raising even mid-study — a brief consultation now may shape your interpretation and your next experiment more than the choice between ANCOM-BC2 and MaAsLin2.
