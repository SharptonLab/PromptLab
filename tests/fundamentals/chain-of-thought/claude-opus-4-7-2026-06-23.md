# Statistical Approach for Microbiome Data Analysis

## Step 1 — Data Structure

Key features that constrain the analysis:

1. **Compositional outcome**: Relative abundances are constrained to sum to 1 (or 100%). This means taxa abundances are *not independent* — an increase in one taxon mathematically forces decreases in others. Standard statistics that assume independent observations on Euclidean space are inappropriate.

2. **High-dimensional**: Many taxa (often 100s–1000s) relative to sample size (n=60 total observations). Risk of overfitting and severe multiple-testing burden.

3. **Sparsity and zero-inflation**: Most microbiome tables have many zeros (taxa absent from many samples). Log-transforms require zero handling.

4. **Repeated measures**: Each mouse sampled at 3 timepoints → within-mouse correlation. Observations within a mouse are not independent.

5. **Cage clustering**: 2–3 mice per cage → within-cage correlation (shared environment, coprophagy is especially relevant for microbiome studies — cage effects are notoriously strong).

6. **Small sample size**: 10 mice/group. Limits power, particularly for complex models and rare taxa.

7. **Hierarchical/nested structure**: Timepoints nested within mice, mice nested within cages, cages nested within treatment (if cages weren't shared across treatments — this is critical to confirm).

8. **Two complementary questions implicit in "community composition"**:
   - **Multivariate / community-level**: Does the overall community differ?
   - **Univariate / taxon-level**: Which specific taxa differ?

---

## Step 2 — Candidate Approaches

**A. PERMANOVA (adonis2 in vegan)**
- Tests whether group centroids differ in multivariate space using a distance matrix (e.g., Bray-Curtis, weighted/unweighted UniFrac, Aitchison).
- Assumes exchangeability under the null; can handle blocking via `strata` or permutation schemes.
- Tests community-level differences.

**B. Mixed-effects models on diversity / ordination axes**
- Reduce community to alpha-diversity indices (Shannon, observed taxa) or first few PCoA/PCA axes, then fit linear mixed model with random effects for mouse and cage.
- Tests univariate summaries over time.

**C. Compositional data analysis (CoDA) approaches**
- Apply centered log-ratio (CLR) or isometric log-ratio (ILR) transform, then use multivariate methods (PERMANOVA on Aitchison distance, or RDA/MANOVA).
- Honors the compositional nature explicitly. Requires zero handling (pseudocounts or Bayesian-multiplicative replacement).

**D. Differential abundance methods (taxon-level)**
- **ANCOM-BC2**, **MaAsLin2**, **LinDA**, **DESeq2** (with caveats), **corncob**.
- MaAsLin2 and LinDA explicitly support random effects (mouse, cage) and longitudinal designs.
- ANCOM-BC2 is robust to compositionality and includes repeated-measures extensions.

**E. Longitudinal microbiome-specific tools**
- **MetaLonDA**, **SplinectomeR**, **NBZIMM**, **ZIBR** for zero-inflated longitudinal modeling.
- Designed for repeated measures of compositional data.

**F. Joint modeling / multivariate longitudinal**
- **GLLVM**, **HMSC**, or multivariate GLMMs — flexible but data-hungry; possibly overkill for n=20 mice.

---

## Step 3 — Assumption Evaluation

| Approach | Problematic assumptions for your data |
|---|---|
| **PERMANOVA** | Standard adonis2 assumes independent observations. With repeated measures + cage, you need permutation **constrained within mouse** (for time) and **stratified by cage**. Also sensitive to dispersion differences (run `betadisper` as a check). |
| **Mixed model on diversity** | Diversity indices throw away most information. Time may not be linear → may need splines or treat as factor. Random effects structure (mouse within cage) must be specified correctly; with only ~7–10 cages, cage random effect estimation is shaky. |
| **CoDA + multivariate** | Zero handling is non-trivial and choice affects results. Interpretation of ILR coordinates is non-intuitive. |
| **MaAsLin2 / LinDA / ANCOM-BC2** | Most handle random effects, but small sample size limits power for FDR-corrected taxon-level discoveries. Cage random effect may not converge well with few cages. |
| **Longitudinal-specific tools** | Generally appropriate but many assume specific zero-inflation distributions that may not fit; software maturity varies. |

**The cage issue is the most worrying.** If treatments were assigned at the *cage* level (all mice in a cage got the same treatment), then **cage is the experimental unit, not mouse** — your effective n could be as low as 7–10 cages total, not 20 mice. This dramatically changes the analysis.

---

## Step 4 — Practical Considerations

- **Field conventions**: PERMANOVA (Bray-Curtis or UniFrac) + a differential abundance method is the standard reporting combination in microbiome papers. Reviewers expect both.
- **Software maturity**: vegan (PERMANOVA), MaAsLin2, ANCOM-BC2, and phyloseq are well-supported R packages with extensive documentation.
- **Interpretability**: PCoA plots + PERMANOVA are easy to communicate; CoDA approaches are statistically cleaner but harder to explain.
- **Reproducibility**: Stick with widely-used tools so others can replicate.
- **Visualization**: PCoA/NMDS with trajectories, stacked bar plots, and heatmaps will accompany whichever test you choose.

---

## Step 5 — Recommendation

I would suggest discussing a **two-pronged analysis** with your statistician:

**Community-level**: PERMANOVA on Bray-Curtis (and ideally also Aitchison) distances, with permutations **constrained within mouse** for the time factor and **stratified/blocked by cage**. Pair with `betadisper` to distinguish location vs. dispersion effects, and PCoA plots with trajectories per mouse.

**Taxon-level**: **MaAsLin2** or **LinDA** with treatment × time as fixed effects and random effects for mouse (nested in cage). These handle longitudinal compositional data and are field-standard.

**Critical questions to ask the statistician:**

1. **Cage structure**: Were treatments randomized at the cage level or mouse level? Were any cages mixed-treatment? This determines whether cage is a nuisance random effect or the experimental unit — and may dramatically reduce my effective sample size.
2. **Random effects**: With only ~7–10 cages, can I reliably estimate a cage random effect, or should I use cage as a fixed blocking factor / use cluster-robust standard errors instead?
3. **Time modeling**: Should day be treated as a categorical factor (3 levels) or continuous (linear/spline)? With only 3 timepoints, categorical is usually safer.
4. **Distance metric choice**: Bray-Curtis vs. weighted UniFrac vs. Aitchison — which is most defensible given my biological question (do I care about presence/absence, abundance, or phylogenetic relatedness)?
5. **Baseline handling**: Should day 0 be a covariate (baseline-adjusted) or included as part of the trajectory? Baseline adjustment usually increases power if groups differ at baseline by chance.
6. **Power**: Given n=10 mice/group with cage clustering, what effect sizes am I realistically powered to detect, and should I pre-specify a few taxa of interest to reduce multiple-testing burden?
7. **Dispersion check**: How should I report and interpret `betadisper` results if dispersion differs between groups?

Bring your metadata file (with cage assignments) and a small example OTU/ASV table to the meeting — the cage question is the single biggest determinant of how to proceed.
