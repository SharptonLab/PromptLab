1.  **Longitudinal / Repeated Measures:** You have samples from the same mouse at three different time points. This means the observations are not independent; the sample from mouse #1 at day 14 is related to the sample from mouse #1 at day 0. Your statistical model *must* account for this non-independence to avoid pseudo-replication and inflated false positives.

2.  **Compositional Data:** The outcome variable is relative abundance. This is the most critical feature. Compositional data has two properties that violate the assumptions of many standard statistical tests (like ANOVA or t-tests):
    *   **Sum-to-Constant Constraint:** All abundances in a sample sum to 1 (or 100%).
    *   **Non-independence:** If the relative abundance of one bacterium goes up, the relative abundance of at least one other bacterium *must* go down.

3.  **Nested/Clustered Data (Cage Effects):** Mice co-housed in the same cage are not independent replicates. They share an environment, diet, and can exchange microbes through coprophagy. This is often handled by treating "cage" as a random effect.

4.  **Interaction Effect:** Your core question—"Does probiotic treatment affect community composition *over time*?"—is a question about an **interaction** between `Treatment` and `Time`. ### **Step 2 - Candidate approaches: What statistical approaches could address my question?**

Here are the most common and appropriate candidate approaches for this type of question.

1.  **Permutational Multivariate Analysis of Variance (PERMANOVA):**
    *   **What it is:** A non-parametric method that tests for differences in community composition between groups based on a dissimilarity matrix (e.g., Bray-Curtis, Jaccard, or UniFrac distances).
    *   **What it assumes:** It assumes that groups have similar multivariate dispersion (spread). It does not assume normality. The significance is determined by permuting sample labels to generate a null distribution.
    *   **What it tests:** It tests the null hypothesis that the centroids (multivariate means) of the groups are equivalent. A significant result means that, overall, the community composition is different between the groups you are testing. It is highly flexible and can model complex designs, including interactions and random effects.

2.  **Compositional Data Analysis (CoDa) with Linear Mixed-Effects Models (LMM):**
    *   **What it is:** This approach first transforms the relative abundance data to unconstrained, real numbers using a log-ratio transformation (e.g., Centered Log-Ratio, CLR). Then, a standard statistical model, like a Linear Mixed-Effects Model, is applied to the transformed data.
    *   **What it assumes:** It assumes the data can be adequately transformed to meet the assumptions of the LMM (e.g., normality of residuals). A major challenge is handling zeros, as the logarithm of zero is undefined.

    *   **What it assumes:** Varies by tool (e.g., ANCOM-BC, DESeq2, ALDEx2), but modern tools are designed to handle compositional, overdispersed count data and can often incorporate mixed-effects models.
