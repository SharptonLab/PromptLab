# Statistical Approach Selection for Your Microbiome Study

## Step 1 — Data Structure: Key Features

Your data has several features that constrain analysis choices:

1. **Compositional outcome**: Relative abundances sum to 1 (or 100%) per sample. This induces *spurious negative correlations* between taxa — if one goes up, others must go down. Standard statistics on raw proportions are invalid.
2. **High dimensionality**: Typically hundreds to thousands of taxa, often with many zeros (sparsity). Likely *p >> n*.
3. **Repeated measures**: 3 time points per mouse → within-mouse correlation.
4. **Nested/clustered design**: Mice within cages → cage-level correlation (shared microbes via coprophagy is well-documented in mice and often *larger* than treatment effects).
5. **Two-factor design**: Treatment (between) × Time (within).
6. **Small n**: 20 mice limits power, especially for complex models.
7. **Two related questions**:
   - **Community-level**: Does overall composition differ? (multivariate)
   - **Taxon-level**: Which specific taxa change? (per-feature testing)

## Step 2 — Candidate Approaches

### Community-level (multivariate)

| Approach | What it tests | Key assumptions |
|---|---|---|
| **PERMANOVA** (adonis2) | Differences in centroids of distance matrices between groups | Exchangeability under null; similar dispersion across groups |
| **PERMANOVA with strata/blocks** | Same, but restricts permutations within clusters (cage) | Same, plus correct nesting structure |
| **Mixed-effects PERMANOVA** (e.g., `PERMANOVA-S`, distance-based RDA with conditioning) | Group effects accounting for random effects | Distance choice is appropriate; correct random-effects structure |
| **MiRKAT / GLMM-MiRKAT** | Kernel-based association with covariates and random effects | Kernel choice reflects biology |

### Taxon-level (per-feature)

| Approach | What it tests | Key assumptions |
|---|---|---|
| **ANCOM-BC2** | Differential abundance with bias correction; supports repeated measures and random effects | Log-linear model on bias-corrected abundance |
| **MaAsLin2/3** | GLMM-style per-taxon testing with fixed + random effects | Distributional assumption (log-transformed, ZINB, etc.) |
| **LinDA** | Linear models on CLR-transformed data with bias correction | Linearity after transformation |
| **DESeq2 with random effects (limma duplicateCorrelation)** | Negative binomial GLM per taxon | NB distribution; designed for counts, not relative abundance |

### Effect over time

- **Time as factor** (treatment × time interaction) — most common
- **Trajectory-based** — splines or GAMMs per taxon, more powerful if trends are smooth

## Step 3 — Assumption Evaluation for Your Data

- **PERMANOVA (basic)**: Will be invalid if you ignore cage and repeated measures — pseudo-replication will inflate Type I error. *Must* handle nesting.
- **PERMANOVA dispersion assumption**: Probiotic treatment often changes *variability* in composition (e.g., placebo group more variable). Run `betadisper` to check; if violated, significant PERMANOVA can reflect dispersion differences, not centroid shifts.
- **Distance choice**: Bray-Curtis, weighted/unweighted UniFrac, Aitchison (CLR-based, compositionally aware) all give different answers. Aitchison distance respects compositionality.
- **MaAsLin2/ANCOM-BC2**: Per-taxon GLMMs assume enough non-zero observations per taxon — sparse taxa will fail or give unstable estimates. With only 60 samples, random effects for cage *and* mouse may be unstable.
- **Cage effect identifiability**: If treatment is assigned at the cage level (all mice in a cage get same treatment — common!), cage and treatment are **confounded**. You cannot statistically separate them. Verify how randomization was done.
- **Small n**: Permutation tests are fine but limited resolution; mixed models may have convergence issues.

## Step 4 — Practical Considerations

- **Field conventions**: Microbiome papers typically report (a) alpha diversity changes, (b) beta diversity with PERMANOVA, (c) differential abundance with ANCOM-BC2 or MaAsLin2. Reviewers will expect this.
- **Software maturity**: `vegan` (PERMANOVA), `MaAsLin2/3`, `ANCOM-BC2`, `phyloseq`, `mia`/`miaTime` are well-supported in R. QIIME2 wraps many of these.
- **Multiple testing**: Per-taxon analyses need FDR correction across hundreds of taxa.
- **Interpretability**: PERMANOVA gives one global p-value (easy to report). Differential abundance gives a taxon list (more biological insight).
- **Reproducibility**: Compositional methods (ANCOM-BC2, LinDA) are increasingly preferred over older approaches (Wilcoxon on relative abundance, edgeR/DESeq2) per benchmarking studies (Nearing et al. 2022; Yang & Chen 2022).

## Step 5 — Recommendation and Questions for Your Statistician

### Suggested analytical plan (to discuss, not adopt blindly)

1. **Diagnostics first**:
   - Verify how treatment was randomized relative to cages.
   - Check sample sizes per taxon; filter low-prevalence taxa (e.g., present in <10–20% of samples).
   - Run `betadisper` for each time point.

2. **Community-level**: PERMANOVA on Aitchison and Bray-Curtis distances, with `strata = cage` (or restricted permutations within mouse), testing treatment × time interaction. Report dispersion results alongside.

3. **Taxon-level**: **MaAsLin2 or ANCOM-BC2** with:
   - Fixed effects: treatment, time, treatment×time
   - Random effects: mouse nested in cage (if not confounded)
   - FDR correction across taxa

4. **Alpha diversity**: Linear mixed model (Shannon, observed features) with the same random-effects structure.

### Questions to ask your statistician

1. **Is treatment confounded with cage in our design?** If yes, what can we still estimate?
2. **Given only 20 mice in 7–10 cages, is the random-effects structure (mouse within cage) estimable, or will models be singular?** Should cage be a fixed effect instead?
3. **How should we handle the treatment × time interaction with so few time points** — categorical time or a trend test?
4. **Which distance metric best matches our biological question** (presence/absence vs. abundance shifts; phylogeny-aware or not)?
5. **If `betadisper` shows unequal dispersion, how should we interpret PERMANOVA results?**
6. **What's the appropriate prevalence/abundance filter** before differential abundance testing, given our sample size?
7. **Should we pre-register the analysis plan** to avoid garden-of-forking-paths issues with so many method choices?
8. **Do we have power to detect realistic effect sizes**, or should this be framed as exploratory/hypothesis-generating?

The honest answer for n=20 with cage clustering is that this study is likely **modestly powered for community-level effects** and **exploratory for individual taxa**. Framing matters as much as the test choice.
