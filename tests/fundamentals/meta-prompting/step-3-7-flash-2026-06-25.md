#### 1. Ambiguous or Underspecified Elements
The original prompt leaves critical details unstated that make it impossible to generate a targeted, accurate analysis:
- **No definition of "important"**: There are no criteria for statistical significance (e.g., adjusted p-value cutoff), biological significance (e.g., minimum magnitude of expression change), or functional relevance (e.g., liver-specific genes, metabolic enzymes, transcription factors).
- **No input data specifications**: The format of the RNA-seq results is not defined (e.g., CSV of DESeq2 output, list of gene symbols, raw counts?), nor is the gene identifier system (Ensembl, MGI symbols, RefSeq?) or included columns (log2 fold change, p-values, normalized counts?) specified.
- **No study or preprocessing context**: There is no information on sample size, study design (paired/unpaired, HFD duration, covariates like age/sex included in the differential expression [DE] model), preprocessing steps (normalization method, low-count gene filtering, batch correction), or DE pipeline used (DESeq2, edgeR, etc.).
- **No output requirements**: There is no guidance on whether results should be split by up/down regulation, ranked, annotated with functional context, or tied to a specific use case (e.g., therapeutic targets, biomarker discovery).
- **No explicit instruction to share the actual data**: The prompt references "my RNA-seq results" but does not require the user to provide them, making analysis impossible without additional input.

#### 2. Unstated Assumptions That May Not Hold
The prompt relies on several implicit assumptions that are often incorrect:
- Assumption that "important" is a universal, objective category, when it is entirely dependent on the user’s research goal (e.g., a gene with a small but statistically significant fold change may be critical for basic mechanism studies, while a gene with a large fold change may be more relevant for clinical biomarker development).
- Assumption that the RNA-seq data is already properly processed: no unaddressed batch effects, correct multiple testing correction, appropriate low-count filtering, and accurate gene annotation. The model cannot verify this without explicit context.
- Assumption that the model has access to the user’s proprietary results, which is not true unless the user explicitly shares the data in the conversation.
- Assumption that the model has perfect, up-to-date knowledge of mouse liver biology and HFD literature, which may have gaps for newly annotated genes or niche research contexts.
- Assumption that the user does not need to account for analysis limitations (e.g., small sample size, unmeasured confounders) that could undermine the reliability of DE results.

#### 3. Potential Failure Modes
This prompt will likely produce low-quality, unreliable, or irrelevant outputs:
- **Hallucination**: If the user does not provide actual results, the model will generate a generic list of well-known HFD liver genes (e.g., *Pparg*, *Fasn*) that may not be present in the user’s specific dataset.
- **Invalid statistical thresholds**: The model may use unadjusted p-values (e.g., p < 0.05) instead of adjusted p-values to account for multiple testing, leading to high false positive rates. It may also use arbitrary fold change cutoffs that are not biologically justified for the user’s study.
- **Misannotation of genes**: If the user provides non-standard gene IDs, the model may incorrectly map them to gene symbols or functions.
- **Prioritization based on popularity over effect size**: The model may list well-studied HFD genes first even if they have trivial effect sizes in the user’s data, while ignoring novel, high-effect genes.
- **Lack of actionable context**: The model may output a bare list of gene symbols with no explanation of their role in liver metabolism or HFD pathophysiology, making the output useless for interpretation.
- **Misattribution of differential expression**: Without context on covariates and batch correction, the model may report genes that are differential due to technical artifacts (e.g., batch) rather than HFD exposure.
- **Overinterpretation**: The model may make causal claims about gene function that are not supported by observational DE data.

#### 4. Improvements to the Prompt
To make outputs consistent, reliable, and actionable:
- Explicitly require the user to share their DE results in a specified format, with clear gene identifiers and core DE metrics.
- Explicitly define "important" with customizable, transparent thresholds for statistical and biological significance, tied to the user’s research goal if specified.
- Require the user to provide key study and preprocessing context to allow the model to flag limitations and avoid misinterpreting results.
- Specify a clear output structure that includes ranking, functional annotation, and limitation notes.
