# 1. What is ambiguous or underspecified?

### “RNA-seq results” is not defined
It is unclear what data will be provided. Possible inputs include:

- Raw counts
- Normalized counts
- DESeq2/edgeR/limma differential expression table
- TPM/FPKM values
- Volcano plot
- Gene set enrichment results
- A list of significant genes only

The appropriate interpretation depends heavily on the data type.

### “Important genes” is vague
“Important” could mean:

- Most statistically significant genes
- Largest fold-change genes
- Genes passing FDR and effect-size thresholds
- Genes biologically relevant to liver metabolism
- Genes associated with high-fat diet, steatosis, inflammation, fibrosis, or insulin resistance
- Genes suitable for validation by qPCR
- Genes that are potential biomarkers or therapeutic targets

Without defining importance, the model may rank genes inconsistently.

### The statistical criteria are missing
The prompt does not specify thresholds such as:

- Adjusted p-value/FDR cutoff, e.g. `padj < 0.05`
- Absolute log2 fold-change cutoff, e.g. `|log2FC| > 1`

# 2. What assumptions are being made that might not hold?

### Assumption: statistically significant genes are biologically important
A gene can have a very small effect size but be statistically significant, especially with many replicates. Conversely, biologically meaningful genes may miss significance due to noise or low power.

### Assumption: largest fold-change genes are most important
Genes with very large fold changes can be lowly expressed, noisy, or poorly annotated. They may not be the best biological drivers.

### Assumption: high-fat diet effects are uniform
High-fat diet effects depend on mouse strain, sex, diet duration, diet composition, housing, microbiome, and metabolic phenotype. A 4-week diet and a 24-week diet may produce very different liver transcriptional responses.

### Assumption: liver RNA reflects one cell type
Whole-liver RNA-seq includes hepatocytes, Kupffer cells, stellate cells, endothelial cells, immune infiltrates, and other cell populations. Differential expression may reflect changes in cell composition rather than regulation within a cell type.

### Assumption: gene names are interpretable and current
Mouse gene identifiers may be outdated, duplicated, or ambiguous. Some symbols may map poorly to current annotations.

### Assumption: the contrast is clear
“High-fat diet compared to control” needs directionality. For example, does positive log2FC mean higher in high-fat diet, or higher in control?

### Assumption: there are no confounders
Batch, sex, age, cage, litter, sequencing lane, RNA integrity, and sample quality can all confound differential expression.

### Assumption: the AI can infer causality
Differential expression does not prove that a gene drives the phenotype. It only indicates association with the condition under the study design.

---

# 3. What failure modes might this prompt produce?

### Hallucinated gene prioritization
If no actual differential expression table is provided, the model may produce a generic list of “expected” high-fat-diet liver genes, such as `Pparg`, `Srebf1`, `Fasn`, `Cd36`, `Ccl2`, or `Tnf`, whether or not they are significant in the user’s dataset.

### Overinterpretation
The model may claim that genes are “causal,” “drivers,” or “therapeutic targets” without sufficient evidence.

### Ignoring multiple testing
The model might rank genes by nominal p-value rather than adjusted p-value, producing false positives.

### Confusing direction of change
If the contrast direction is not specified, the model could incorrectly label genes as upregulated in high-fat diet versus control.

### Overweighting fold change
The model may prioritize genes with extreme log2FC values that are lowly expressed or unreliable.

### Missing pathway-level patterns
A gene-by-gene interpretation may miss stronger biological signals at the pathway level, such as lipid metabolism, inflammatory response, oxidative stress, bile acid metabolism, fibrosis, or mitochondrial function.

### Producing inconsistent output
Different runs may use different definitions of “important,” different thresholds, or different ranking logic.

### Not identifying data quality issues
The model may proceed with interpretation even if the input table lacks essential columns, has uncorrected p-values, or includes very low-count genes.

### Species confusion
The model may interpret mouse genes as human genes or use human-centric literature without noting orthology differences.

---

# 4. How to improve the prompt

A stronger prompt should:

1. Specify the input format and required columns.
2. Define what “important” means.
3. Provide statistical thresholds.
4. Specify the contrast direction.
5. Ask the model to distinguish statistical evidence from biological interpretation.
6. Require caveats and avoid causal claims.
7. Request a reproducible ranking method.
8. Include relevant metadata.
9. Define the desired output format.
10. Ask for uncertainty or flags when information is insufficient.

## Improved prompt

I will provide a differential expression results table from a mouse liver bulk RNA-seq study comparing high-fat diet versus control diet.

Please analyze the table and prioritize genes that are most relevant to the high-fat diet response.

### Study context

- Species: mouse, liver tissue
- Comparison: high-fat diet versus control diet
- Positive `log2FoldChange` means higher expression in high-fat diet relative to control
- Differential expression method: [DESeq2/edgeR/limma; specify if known]
- Biological replicates: [insert n per group]
- Mouse strain/sex/age/diet duration if known: [insert metadata]
- Covariates included in the model: [batch, sex, age, etc., or “none/unknown”]

### Input table

The table includes the following columns:

- `gene_id`
- `gene_symbol`
- `baseMean` or mean expression
- `log2FoldChange`
- `lfcSE`, if available
- `pvalue`
- `padj` or FDR

If any essential columns are missing, tell me what is missing before interpreting the results.

### Criteria for prioritizing genes

Define “important” genes using the following hierarchy:

1. Genes with `padj < 0.05` and `abs(log2FoldChange) >= 1`
2. Among those, prioritize genes with stronger adjusted significance, larger effect size, and sufficient expression level
3. Also highlight genes with known relevance to liver metabolism, lipid handling, inflammation, fibrosis, oxidative stress, mitochondrial function, bile acid metabolism, or insulin resistance
4. Do not treat differential expression as proof of causality

### Requested output

Please provide:

1. A brief quality check of the table, including whether the needed columns are present and whether the contrast direction is clear.
2. A ranked table of the top upregulated genes in high-fat diet.
3. A ranked table of the top downregulated genes in high-fat diet.
4. A short biological interpretation of the major patterns.
5. Any notable pathways or functional categories represented by the prioritized genes.
6. A list of 5–10 candidate genes for follow-up validation by qPCR, with reasons for each.
7. Caveats, including possible confounding by cell-type composition, batch effects, sex, strain, diet duration, and multiple testing.
8. If the data do not support a conclusion, say so explicitly rather than speculating.

Please base your conclusions only on the provided table and clearly separate data-supported findings from general biological background.

---

This revised prompt makes the task more constrained, reduces the risk of hallucinated gene lists, clarifies what “important” means, and encourages a more reproducible interpretation.
