## 2. What assumptions are you making that might not hold?

### Assumption: the most significant genes are the most biologically important
Genes with very small FDR values are not always the most biologically meaningful. Highly expressed genes can achieve strong statistical significance even with modest effect sizes.

### Assumption: large fold changes are always meaningful
Genes with large fold changes may be lowly expressed, noisy, or based on sparse counts.

### Assumption: gene symbols are unambiguous
Mouse gene symbols can be outdated, duplicated, or confused with human orthologs. Some gene identifiers may need mapping.

### Assumption: differential expression alone identifies causal genes
RNA-seq identifies associations, not necessarily causal regulators. A differentially expressed gene may be downstream of the phenotype rather than driving it.

### Assumption: liver tissue reflects one homogeneous cell population
Bulk liver RNA-seq reflects a mixture of hepatocytes, Kupffer cells, stellate cells, endothelial cells, immune cells, and others. Apparent gene-expression changes may reflect changes in cell composition.

### Assumption: high-fat diet effects are uniform
Different high-fat diet protocols can produce different outcomes: simple steatosis, NASH-like inflammation, fibrosis, insulin resistance, or mild metabolic adaptation.

### Assumption: the statistical model is appropriate
The results depend on whether the differential expression analysis controlled for sex, batch, cage, litter, sequencing depth, and other covariates.

### Assumption: the model can infer everything from the result table
A gene list alone may not reveal experimental design flaws, batch effects, or confounders.

## 3. What failure modes might this prompt produce?

The model may overstate the relevance of genes based on general knowledge, even if the data do not support strong conclusions.

### Inconsistent ranking criteria

### Ignoring multiple-testing correction
The model might treat nominal p-values as significant without checking FDR.

### Confusing upregulated and downregulated genes
